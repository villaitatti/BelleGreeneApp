The Letters of
Belle da Costa Greene
to Bernard Berenson

Traveling the long distance from New York City to Florence, the letters of Belle Greene to Bernard Berenson document one of the art world’s most extraordinary relationships.

Belle Greene ran the Morgan Library for forty-three years—initially as the private librarian of J. Pierpont Morgan and then his son, Jack, and later as the inaugural director of the Pierpont Morgan Library (now the Morgan Library & Museum).

She attracted considerable attention both in the press and from her many admirers, among them the Italian Renaissance art historian, scholar, and connoisseur Bernard Berenson (1865–1959).

The Letters

Following their introduction in early 1909, Greene and Berenson maintained a decades-long epistolary relationship, occasionally sharing time together in Europe or America: they were lovers, but also friends and intellectual peers.

While Greene seems to have destroyed Berenson’s letters to her, hers to him remain at I Tatti, the former home of Bernard and his wife, Mary, on the outskirts of Florence, now the Harvard University Center for Italian Renaissance Studies.

These letters, which span the period February 1909 to March 1949, constitute by far the most substantial surviving archive of personal papers associated with Greene.

In October 1910 Greene makes a telling comment about her letters, suggesting that Berenson wanted her to include a diary-like level of detail in her correspondence to him: “You see dear, how I am living up to your desire of a diary but for God’s Sake let me know it bores you.” It has been erroneously said that Greene referred to her letters as “her diary,” when in fact she mentions an actual diary, running to at least ten volumes, that she began using in the fall of 1909.

In this letter, Greene describes her diary to Berenson and asserts that she “shall never allow a living soul to see it + for that reason I write things I hardly dare think.” Elsewhere she says her diary functions “as though it were another Belle.” The diaries, unlike these letters, do not survive today, and were presumably destroyed by Greene before her death.
BELLE GREENE’S DIARY
9/12/1909

Three years before the epochal Armory Show exhibition of modern art, Belle da Costa Greene was viewing Henri Matisse drawings in Manhattan. This letter recounts, in part, Greene’s visit to the Photo Secession, or 291 Gallery, “where [her] two dearly beloved’s Stieglitz + Steichen hold forth.” Here, in March 1910, she saw an early exhibition of Henri Matisse drawings (she would buy one in 1912). She regarded the Matisse exhibition with conflicting emotions: “They are the most extraordinary things I have ever seen.

it was absolutely new to me and almost incomprehensible. I don’t understand what he is trying to do, what he is wanting to make me think whether an emphasis of character (sometimes called caricature) although at first it appeared to me to be only a series of extravagant contortions. They shocked + repelled me strongly at first but held me there quite two hours.” Greene would own works of modern art by creators such as Abraham Walkowitz and Clara Tice, contribute a short piece to the journal Camera Work, and have interesting encounters with Marcel Duchamp and Francis Picabia, though she did not herself contribute to the cultural movement of American modernism.
